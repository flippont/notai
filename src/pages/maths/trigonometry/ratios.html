Trigonometric ratios are the ratios of the lengths of sides of a right-angled triangle. There are three types of trigonometric ratios.

The three sides of a right angled triangle is called the hypotenuse (the longest side), the adjacent (The side that is next to a given angle) and the opposite (The side that is the opposite to a given angle)

The Sine (sin)

Sin(θ) is the height of a triangle, where the hypotenuse is length 1 and the angle is equal to x degrees. For example, sin (10) is equal to 0.173, because the height of a right-angled triangle where the angle is 10 degrees, and the hypotenuse length is 1 is equal to 0.173. When you want to apply it to a triangle where the hypotenuse is larger or smaller than 1, you multiply the sin(x) by the hypotenuse.

Hence, the formula would be:

Sin θ = Opposite/Hypotenuse

The Cosine (cos)

cos(θ) is the base of a triangle, where the hypotenuse is length 1 and the angle is equal to x degrees. For example, cos (10) is equal to 0.984, because the base of a right-angled triangle where the angle is 10 degrees, and the hypotenuse length is 1 is equal to 0.984. When you want to apply it to a triangle where the hypotenuse is larger or smaller than 1, you multiply the cos(θ) by the hypotenuse.

Hence, the formula would be:

Cos θ = Adjacent/Hypotenuse

The Tangent (tan)

tan(θ) is the height of a triangle, where the adjacent is length 1 and the angle is equal to x degrees. For example, tan (10) is equal to 0.176, because the height of a right-angled triangle where the angle is 10 degrees, and the adjacent length is 1 is equal to 0.176. When you want to apply it to a triangle where the adjacent is larger or smaller than 1, you multiply the tan(θ) by the adjacent.

Hence, the formula would be:

Tan θ = Opposite/Adjacent

 

The formulas are often remembered with SOHCAHTOA standing for each of the first letters in the element.

The theory of Pythagoras states that

(the adjacent)2 + (the opposite)2 = (the hypotenuse)2

So (sin θ)2 + (cos θ)2 = 1
