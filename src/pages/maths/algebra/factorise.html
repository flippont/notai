Expanding brackets involves multiplying every term inside the bracket by the term that is outside of the bracket.

When you want to expand a single bracket the formula you use is

a(b + c) = ab + ac

The formula for expanding multiple brackets is

(a + b) (c + d)
= a(c + d) + b(c + d)
= ac + ad + bc + bd

This is process is the same as the distributive law.

The distributive law is a law in maths, where: for natural numbers A, x1, x2…xn you can say -

Ax1 + Ax2 + Ax3 + … + Axn = A(x1 + x2 + x3 + … + xn)

For example,

6 × 3 + 6 × 7 + 6 × 10
= 6 × (3 + 7 + 10)
= 6 × (20)
= 120

 

Factorising is the opposite of expanding brackets, where you multiply out a bracket to find the answer, and is used to solve quadratic equations.

There are mainly 4 ways to factorise an equation (Grouping, Highest Common factor, Quadratic and Difference of two squares).

Highest Common factor

The Highest common factor of two or more numbers / monomials is the product of all their common factors. For example, the highest common factor of 8x and 4x is 4x.

Using this, we can use the distributive law to factor out the common factor.

In the equation 4x + 8x, the common factor is 4x, so if we factor that out we get 4x ×  (1 + 2) or 4x(1 + 2).

Grouping

After finding the highest common factor, we can sometimes be left with a situation where we can further use the distributive law. This is called grouping.

For example, when we have the equation –

ab + ay + xb + xy

and find the highest common factor for all terms, we are left with -

a (b + y) + x (b + y)

As you can see, both terms have (b + y) included, so you can treat that as a highest common factor and factor out b + y from the full equation.

If we do that, we are left with -

(a + x) (x + y)

This is the principle of Grouping.

Quadratic
A quadratic is a polynomial that is in the form of

ax2 + bx + c

where a is the coefficient (Number in front) of x2, b is the coefficient of the x and the c is the constant (Number on its own). We will use 2x2 + 5x + 3 as an example.

There are a few steps involved in factorising a quadratic.

  1. Multiply a and c and write out the factors of (c × a), which we will now call “m”
    m = 6
  2. Find a pair of factors that add together to make b, the middle number and multiply to get m.
    3 + 2 = 5 = b, 3 * 2 = 6 = m
  3. Go back to the original polynomial, and now split the middle term into the two factors we found in step 2.
    2x2 + 2x + 3x + 3
  4. Now group the four sets of terms.
    2x(x + 2) + 3 (x + 1) = (2x + 3)(x + 1)

DOTS

Difference Of Two Squares, or DOTS is a method of factorising that works in a very specific case, when an expression is made up of the difference of two perfect squares, i.e. a2 + b2.

Then we can factor it as (a + b) (a - b).

For example, x2 – 36 can be factorised as (x + 6) (x - 6)
